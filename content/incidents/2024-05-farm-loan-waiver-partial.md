By May 2024 — two months past the promised six-month deadline — the Telangana government had disbursed approximately ₹2,000 crore of the estimated ₹31,000 crore total crop loan waiver commitment. Agriculture Minister Thummala Nageswara Rao acknowledged the slow pace in a press briefing but attributed it to the complexity of reconciling data across nationalised banks, regional rural banks, cooperative societies, and PACS.

Farmers in Nalgonda district, a cotton and paddy-growing belt with high levels of institutional borrowing, held protests at the district cooperative bank and at the office of the local MLA. Farmer unions noted that many growers with loans at cooperative banks had not received any waiver amount, despite the six-month deadline passing. Some farmers had already received bank notices for overdue interest — a situation the waiver was supposed to prevent.

The Agriculture Department clarified that priority had been given to farmers with loans at nationalised banks, where data integration was more straightforward, and that cooperative bank beneficiaries would be covered in subsequent tranches. No binding timeline was offered. Opposition parties seized on the figures to argue that the Congress government had made an unaffordable commitment during the election campaign, while the government maintained the full ₹2 lakh waiver would eventually be implemented.
